# Zone 1: The Sanctuary (Retreat Center)

## Overview

**Full Name:** The Sanctuary of Infinite Becoming (formerly a bankrupt golf resort)

A residential retreat center nestled in the mountains / jungle / countryside (TBD). From the outside it looks like a wellness spa. Inside, it's a feudal society with a caste system more rigid than medieval Europe — but everyone insists it's "non-hierarchical."

Steve arrives because someone at his wife's yoga studio said she "went deep at The Sanctuary." He books what he thinks is a weekend getaway. He's wrong.

## The Caste System

The Sanctuary operates on a strict social hierarchy that nobody acknowledges:

### Tier 1: The Founders / Elders
- Run the place. Set the rules. Take no feedback.
- Have been "on their journey" the longest, which apparently grants moral authority
- Live in private cabins while everyone else shares dorms
- Their word is spiritual law, delivered as "invitations" that are actually commands
- "I'm not telling you what to do, I'm just reflecting back what I see" = I'm telling you what to do

### Tier 2: The Facilitators
- Lead workshops, ceremonies, and sessions
- Paid in "energy exchange" (not money, definitely not money)
- Compete viciously for status while preaching non-attachment
- Each one thinks their modality is the real one
- Passive-aggressive turf wars over workshop time slots

### Tier 3: The Paying Guests
- The actual revenue source. Treated like fragile children who need guidance
- Pay $200-500/night to sleep in shared rooms and eat unseasoned food
- Told their discomfort is "resistance" and their complaints are "projections"
- The more they pay, the more "advanced" the programs they can access

### Tier 4: The Karma Yogis (Volunteers)
- Work 6-8 hours a day in exchange for attending workshops
- Do all the actual labor: cooking, cleaning, building, maintenance
- Told this is "seva" (selfless service) and a spiritual practice
- Cannot complain because complaining means they "aren't surrendered"
- Some have been here for months. Some for years. Some have forgotten what money is.
- Steve is horrified. This is just unpaid labor with incense.

### Invisible Tier: The Locals
- People from the nearby town who work the jobs nobody else will do
- Paid minimum wage, not included in "community," treated as background NPCs
- Know exactly what's going on and are the most clear-eyed people in the game
- Great source of side quests and honest information

## The Physical Space

### Areas (Game Map)

**The Welcome Pavilion** — Entry point. Registration desk staffed by a Karma Yogi with dead eyes and a radiant smile. Orientation pamphlet is a quest item. Gift shop sells overpriced crystals and branded merchandise.

**The Temple / Main Hall** — Central gathering space. Morning meditation, evening sharing circles, workshops during the day. Smells like nag champa permanently. Shoes are forbidden. Steve's socks have holes.

**The Healing Hut** — Where one-on-one sessions happen. Crystal healing, reiki, somatic therapy, past-life regression. Each healer thinks the others are frauds (but would never say that directly — they'd say "everyone's on their own path").

**The Kitchen / Dining Hall** — Vegan, raw, ayurvedic, or whatever the current food philosophy is. No salt, no sugar, no joy. The Karma Yogis cook for 100+ people and eat last. Coffee is "available but discouraged." Steve would commit crimes for a cup of normal coffee.

**The Movement Studio** — Yoga, ecstatic dance, breathwork. Multiple classes running simultaneously. The schedule is a nightmare of overlapping time slots and room conflicts.

**The Dorm Rooms** — Shared sleeping quarters. No locks, no privacy, no boundaries. Someone is always processing their emotions loudly at 2am. Steve is assigned a top bunk.

**The Founder's Cabin** — Off-limits. Visible from the path but surrounded by a "please respect the energy of this space" sign. Noticeably nicer than everything else. Has WiFi. (Nobody else has WiFi.)

**The Fire Pit** — Nighttime gathering spot. Guitar circles, sharing circles, someone always has a djembe. This is where rumors and gossip spread, disguised as "witnessing."

**The Sacred Garden** — Meditation area with statues, crystals arranged in grids, prayer flags. Supposedly a place of silence but someone always breaks it to share an "insight."

**The Sweat Lodge / Ceremony Space** — Advanced-access-only area. You need a certain Insight level to enter. This is where the boss encounter happens.

**The Back Office** — Where the actual business runs. Spreadsheets, booking software, marketing plans, influencer outreach. The most corporate place in the building. Steve feels strangely at home here.

## Atmosphere & Tone

- Everything is a ceremony. Meals are ceremonies. Cleaning is a ceremony. The schedule is posted like it's sacred text.
- Nobody uses normal words. Problems are "opportunities." Anger is "activation." Leaving is "completing."
- Passive aggression is weaponized but always delivered with a loving gaze and a hand on the heart.
- The beautiful natural setting is constantly undermined by petty human drama.
- There's always someone crying in a corner and someone else "holding space" for them (watching them cry while looking compassionate).

## How Steve Ends Up Here

Steve googles his wife's last known credit card charges. Finds a payment to "The Sanctuary of Infinite Becoming LLC." Thinks it's a spa. Books a "Taster Weekend." Arrives to find it is not a spa.

## The Wife's Trail

People at The Sanctuary remember Steve's wife well. She came for a weekend. Stayed for two weeks. She "really opened up" during a breathwork session. She started volunteering in the kitchen. Then she met [THE GURU / BOSS CHARACTER] and was "called to go deeper." She left for the next place on the pipeline, carrying a new name and a crystal necklace she didn't have before.

## Key Theme

**The gap between stated values and actual behavior.** Everyone here talks about love, equality, non-judgment, and surrender — while operating a rigid hierarchy, judging everyone constantly, and refusing to hear criticism. The comedy comes from Steve (who is honest about being a boring accountant) being more authentic than the people who've built their identity around authenticity.
